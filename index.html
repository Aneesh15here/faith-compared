The Big Five — Plus One

Every worldview answers three questions

Who (or what) is God? What is wrong with the world? And how is it made right? Every worldview — including atheism — answers all three. Here's each answer in one breath.

✝️

Christianity

Rescue by grace

One God in three persons — Father, Son, Spirit — who pursues people. Humanity's problem is sin; the solution is a free gift: forgiveness through Jesus' death and resurrection, received by faith.

☪️

Islam

Submission to Allah

One transcendent, absolutely singular God. Humanity's problem is failure to submit; the path is the Five Pillars and good deeds outweighing bad at the final judgment.

✡️

Judaism

Covenant faithfulness

One God who covenanted with Israel through Abraham and Moses. Life is lived in faithfulness to Torah — prayer, Sabbath, and study — awaiting the Messianic age.

🕉️

Hinduism

Liberation (moksha)

The divine (Brahman) manifests in many forms. The problem is karma binding souls to the cycle of rebirth; devotion, meditation, or ritual can lead to liberation.

☸️

Buddhism

Cessation (nirvana)

No creator God; the Buddha attained enlightenment, not divinity. Suffering comes from desire and attachment; the Eightfold Path leads toward nirvana, the end of craving.

⚛️

Atheism

No god, self-made meaning

The natural universe is all there is. The "problem" is ignorance and superstition; the path is reason, science, and human flourishing. Meaning is not found but made.

Deep Dive

Christianity ↔ Islam

The world's two largest faiths agree God is one, real, and will judge the world — then part ways on nearly everything that follows. Explore the five great divides, following Nabeel Qureshi's framework in No God but One.

✝️ Christianity

One God, three persons — and his name is Love

The God of the New Testament is triune: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit — one being, eternally existing in loving relationship. That's why the Bible can say "God is love" (1 John 4:8): love existed within God before anything was created.

Because God is relational in himself, he pursues relationship with people — climaxing in sending his Son: "God so loved the world that he gave his one and only Son" (John 3:16).

1 John 4:8John 3:16Matt. 28:19
☪️ Islam

One God, absolutely singular — the Master

Allah is a strict unity (tawhid) — as apologists put it, "a monad" with no internal interpersonal relations. Associating partners with him (shirk) is the gravest sin, so the Trinity and incarnation are firmly rejected (Surah 112).

The Quran describes Allah with 99 names — merciful, compassionate, mighty — but never identifies God as love, nor commands loving God the way the Bible's greatest commandment does. The believer's posture is that of a servant before a master.

Surah 112Surah 4:171Surah 5:73

The hinge: both faiths are monotheistic, but only one says love is eternal within God's own nature. In Islam, God's love would need creation to have an object; in Christianity, the Father loved the Son "before the foundation of the world" (John 17:24).

✝️ Christianity

God the Son, crucified and risen

Jesus is "the Word" who "was God" and "became flesh" (John 1:1, 14) — eternally existing, the agent of creation, "the only begotten of the Father" (John 1:18). He claimed divine authority, accepted worship, and forgave sins.

His crucifixion is the atoning center of history, and his bodily resurrection is Christianity's make-or-break claim: "if Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile" (1 Cor. 15:17).

John 1:1–3Heb. 1:1–21 Cor. 15
☪️ Islam

A great prophet — but only a prophet

The Quran honors Isa (Jesus): virgin-born, miracle-working, even speaking from the cradle (Surah 19:27–34). Yet he is a created being — "the likeness of Jesus is as Adam; He created him from dust" (Surah 3:59).

Crucially, the Quran denies he was crucified: "they killed him not, nor crucified him, but so it was made to appear to them" (Surah 4:157). With no cross, there is no atonement — and Muhammad, not Jesus, is the final prophet and supreme example (Surah 33:21, 40).

Surah 3:59Surah 4:157Surah 19:35

The hinge: the crucifixion. Virtually all historians — religious or not — agree Jesus died by Roman crucifixion; it is among the best-attested facts of ancient history. Islam's denial of it (six centuries after the event) is where apologists press hardest.

✝️ Christianity

A library of testable eyewitness documents

The New Testament is a collection of first-century documents — Gospels, letters, history — that openly appeal to eyewitnesses ("we did not follow cleverly devised myths… we were eyewitnesses," 2 Pet. 1:16) and invite verification (1 Cor. 15:6 names 500 living witnesses).

Evangelical apologists lean into this: even treated merely as historical documents, the manuscripts (5,800+ Greek copies, some within decades of the originals) allow the text to be reconstructed with high confidence — no "corruption" thesis survives the manuscript evidence.

Luke 1:1–42 Pet. 1:161 Cor. 15:3–8
☪️ Islam

One book, one prophet, one recitation

The Quran is held to be Allah's literal, eternal speech, dictated to Muhammad through the angel Gabriel over 23 years. Its literary beauty is offered as its own miracle (the challenge of Surah 2:23).

Islam teaches the earlier revelations — Torah and Injīl (Gospel) — were textually corrupted (tahrif), which is why the Quran was needed. Apologists respond that the Quran itself tells Christians to judge by the Gospel they possessed in Muhammad's day (Surah 5:47) — a Gospel whose manuscripts predate Islam and match today's text.

Surah 2:23Surah 5:47Surah 33:40

The hinge: the corruption claim. Complete New Testament manuscripts (like Codex Sinaiticus, c. AD 350) predate Muhammad by centuries. If the Gospel text in AD 600 is the Gospel text today, the tahrif argument loses its footing — a point Qureshi develops at length.

✝️ Christianity

Grace: rescued, not rewarded

"By grace you have been saved, through faith… it is the gift of God, not a result of works" (Eph. 2:8–9). The gospel's claim is that God himself absorbed the cost of sin at the cross, so forgiveness is free to the guilty and boasting is impossible.

Good works matter — but as the fruit of salvation, not the root. The order is: accepted, therefore obedient. Not: obedient, hoping to be accepted.

Eph. 2:8–10Rom. 3:23–24Titus 3:5
☪️ Islam

The scales: deeds weighed on the last day

The path is submission expressed through the Five Pillars — the confession (Shahada), prayer five times daily (Salat), almsgiving (Zakat), fasting Ramadan (Sawm), and pilgrimage (Hajj).

On the Day of Judgment "those whose scales are heavy — they are the successful; those whose scales are light — they have lost their souls" (Surah 23:102–103). Allah is merciful and may forgive, but there is no substitute, no atoning sacrifice, and no burden-bearer: "no bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another" (Surah 35:18).

Surah 23:102–103Surah 35:18

The hinge: grace versus scales. This is the contrast evangelical writers press most — every religion says "do"; the gospel says "done." One system tells you to climb; the other announces that God came down.

✝️ Christianity

Assurance now, resurrection ahead

Because salvation rests on Christ's finished work rather than fluctuating performance, the believer can have assurance today: "I write these things… that you may know that you have eternal life" (1 John 5:13).

The destination is not a disembodied heaven but bodily resurrection in a renewed creation — Jesus' own resurrection being "the firstfruits" (1 Cor. 15:20) and the guarantee.

1 John 5:131 Cor. 15:20–22Rev. 21:1–4
☪️ Islam

Paradise or fire — decided at the scales

Islam teaches bodily resurrection and a final judgment leading to the gardens of Jannah or the fire of Jahannam. Descriptions of Paradise are vivid and physical — gardens, rivers, rest.

But assurance beforehand is not offered: destiny hangs on the weighing of deeds and Allah's inscrutable decree. Even Muhammad is recorded saying he did not know what would be done with him (Hadith; cf. Surah 46:9). Faithful Muslims live in hope tempered by uncertainty.

Surah 23:102–103Surah 46:9

The hinge: can you know? Apologists note this is often the most personally felt difference in Christian–Muslim conversations: the gospel offers a settled verdict in advance — "there is therefore now no condemnation for those who are in Christ Jesus" (Rom. 8:1).

Weighing the Claims

The evidence question

Christianity plants its flag on a historical event: the resurrection. Gary Habermas's "minimal facts" approach — the standard evangelical apologetic — builds only on data the large majority of scholars, including skeptics, accept.

01

Jesus died by crucifixion

Attested by Christian, Roman (Tacitus), and Jewish (Josephus) sources — accepted by virtually all historians of antiquity.

02

The disciples believed they saw him risen

They proclaimed appearances of the risen Jesus and were transformed from hiding to bold public witness — many at cost of their lives.

03

Paul, the persecutor, converted

A hostile enemy of the church became its greatest missionary, explaining it as an encounter with the risen Christ.

04

James, the skeptic, converted

Jesus' own brother didn't believe during his ministry — then became a leader of the Jerusalem church after a claimed appearance.

05

The tomb was empty

Habermas's "+1": roughly 75% of scholars writing on the subject grant the empty tomb — first reported by women, an unlikely invention in that culture.

Qureshi's five historical questions

In No God but One, former Muslim Nabeel Qureshi asks which faith's central claims survive historical scrutiny.

1

Did Jesus die by crucifixion?

The evidence is overwhelming and near-universally granted — yet the Quran denies it (Surah 4:157), writing six centuries later.

2

Did Jesus rise from the dead?

The minimal facts above demand explanation; Qureshi argues the resurrection explains them better than any naturalistic alternative.

3

Did Jesus claim to be God?

From the earliest sources — Mark's "Son of Man" sayings, Paul's pre-existing creeds — Jesus is presented with divine identity, not as a later legend.

4

Is Muhammad a prophet of God?

Qureshi examines the traditional arguments (character, miracles, prophecies) and finds them resting on late, devotional sources rather than early testimony.

5

Is the Quran the Word of God?

He weighs the literary-miracle claim, alleged scientific foreknowledge, and perfect preservation — and finds each argument weaker than presented.

His conclusion: "The central claims of Christianity are explicitly rejected by Islam" — both cannot be true — and the historical evidence, he argues, decisively favors the Christian claims. Christianity uniquely stakes everything on a checkable public event: "If Christ has not been raised, your faith is futile" (1 Cor. 15:17).

For the Conversation

Questions to ask your Muslim friends

Drawn from David Wood (Acts 17 Apologetics) and Nabeel Qureshi, these questions use the Quran itself as the starting point — not attacks, but genuine puzzles worth thinking through together. Ask them with curiosity, not as "gotchas."

1If the Bible was corrupted, why does the Quran tell Christians to judge by it?

Surah 5:47 commands: "Let the People of the Gospel judge by what Allah has revealed therein." Surah 5:68 says the People of the Book "have nothing" until they observe the Torah and the Gospel. And Surah 10:94 tells doubters to "ask those who have been reading the Scripture before you."

These commands were given in the 7th century — about the Gospel Christians possessed then. But complete New Testament manuscripts (Codex Sinaiticus, Codex Vaticanus, c. AD 325–350) predate Muhammad and match today's text. The Gospel of Muhammad's day is the Gospel of ours.

Why it matters: This is David Wood's "Islamic Dilemma." If the Bible is God's preserved Word, Islam is false (the Bible contradicts the Quran on the cross, resurrection, and Jesus' deity). If the Bible is not God's preserved Word, Islam is still false (the Quran said to judge by it). Either way, the Quran's own endorsement creates the problem.
2If "none can change the words of Allah," how was the Gospel corrupted?

Surah 18:27 declares: "There is no changing His words." Surah 6:115 repeats it: "None can change His words." Yet the Quran also calls the Torah and the Gospel revelations Allah sent down (Surah 5:44–46).

So the question is gentle but pointed: if the Gospel is Allah's word, and no one can change Allah's words, who changed the Gospel — and when, and where is the uncorrupted manuscript trail?

Why it matters: The corruption claim (tahrif) is the load-bearing wall of the Islamic case against Christianity. There is no manuscript evidence of a "different" pre-Islamic Gospel, and the Quran's own preservation promise works against the claim.
3Does the Quran describe the Trinity Christians actually believe?

In Surah 5:116, Allah asks Jesus: "Did you say to the people, 'Take me and my mother as two gods besides Allah?'" — apparently understanding the Trinity as Allah, Jesus, and Mary. Surah 5:73 condemns saying "Allah is the third of three."

But no Christian creed has ever taught that Mary is part of the Trinity, or that there are three gods. Christians believe in one God in three persons — a doctrine settled centuries before Muhammad.

Why it matters: If the Quran is the perfect speech of the all-knowing God, why does it refute a Trinity no Christian believes? A human author in 7th-century Arabia encountering fringe groups might make that mistake; it's harder to explain in a perfect revelation.
4Why does the Quran give Jesus titles it gives no one else — including Muhammad?

In the Quran, Jesus alone is virgin-born (Surah 3:47), called "the Word of Allah and a Spirit from Him" (Surah 4:171), sinless ("a pure boy," Surah 19:19), a healer of the blind and leper who raises the dead (Surah 3:49), alive in heaven now, and returning at the end of history.

Muhammad, by contrast, is told to ask forgiveness for his sin (Surah 47:19; 48:2) and performs no miracle in the Quran itself (Surah 29:50–51).

Why it matters: This was a turning point in Nabeel Qureshi's own journey. If Jesus is uniquely the Word of God, sinless, and alive — even by the Quran's account — isn't he worth investigating on his own terms, in the Gospels?
5How does the Quran's crucifixion denial outweigh all the historical evidence?

Surah 4:157 says of Jesus: "they killed him not, nor crucified him, but so it was made to appear to them." One verse, written six centuries after the event, six hundred miles from Jerusalem.

Against it stand the earliest Christian creeds (within a few years of the event), all four Gospels, Roman historians (Tacitus), Jewish sources (Josephus, the Talmud) — which is why virtually every historian of antiquity, of any religion or none, affirms the crucifixion.

Why it matters: If the "substitution" reading is right, Allah made everyone believe Jesus died — which would make God the author of history's biggest deception, and the unwitting founder of Christianity.
6Can you know, today, that you'll be in Paradise?

Surah 23:102–103 says destiny hangs on the scales of deeds. Surah 19:71 adds a sobering note: "There is not one of you but shall pass over it (Hell)." Even Muhammad said, "I do not know what will be done with me" (cf. Surah 46:9; Sahih al-Bukhari 5.266).

So the personal question: how many good deeds are enough? And if the prophet himself lacked assurance, can anyone have it?

Why it matters: This is usually the most heart-level question of all. The gospel's answer is different in kind: assurance rests on Christ's finished work, not the believer's fluctuating record — "that you may know that you have eternal life" (1 John 5:13).
7If salvation is by deeds, why did God provide a sacrifice for Abraham?

Both faiths tell the story: Abraham is willing to sacrifice his son, and God provides a substitute — "We ransomed him with a momentous sacrifice" (Surah 37:107). Muslims commemorate it every year at Eid al-Adha.

Ask: what did that ransom picture? Why does God's own story establish the principle of a substitute dying in another's place — the very principle Islam denies when it rejects the cross ("no bearer of burdens shall bear the burden of another," Surah 35:18)?

Why it matters: The gospel's claim is that Abraham's ram pointed forward to God's true provision — "Behold, the Lamb of God, who takes away the sin of the world" (John 1:29).

A word on posture: David Wood's debates can be sharp, but the goal in friendship is different — "in your hearts honor Christ the Lord as holy, always being prepared to make a defense… yet do it with gentleness and respect" (1 Peter 3:15). Ask, listen, and let your friend actually wrestle. Most Muslims have never been asked these questions kindly.

When the Questions Come Back

Answering Islamic questions about Christianity

Fair is fair — Muslims have sincere questions for Christians too. Here are the six you'll hear most, with responses drawn from NAMB Apologetics, Answering Islam, and classic evangelical apologetics.

1"The Bible has been corrupted."
The objectionThe original Torah and Injīl were changed over time; that's why the Quran was needed to restore the true message.
A response

Ask the clarifying questions: corrupted when, where, and by whom? We possess 5,800+ Greek New Testament manuscripts, some within decades of the originals, plus complete Bibles (Sinaiticus, Vaticanus) from ~300 years before Muhammad. Manuscripts scattered across three continents could not have been uniformly altered — and they match.

Then note the dilemma: the Quran itself calls the Gospel God's revelation (Surah 5:46), tells Christians to judge by it (5:47), and says none can change Allah's words (18:27). Corruption before Muhammad contradicts the Quran; corruption after Muhammad is refuted by the manuscripts.

2"The Trinity means you worship three gods."
The objectionSaying God is "three" is shirk — associating partners with Allah, the unforgivable sin (Surah 4:48). How can 1 + 1 + 1 = 1?
A response

Start with agreement: Christians also confess one God — "Hear, O Israel: the LORD our God, the LORD is one" (Deut. 6:4; affirmed by Jesus in Mark 12:29). The Trinity is not three gods, and it is not God + Jesus + Mary (the view Surah 5:116 refutes).

The key distinction is being vs. person: one divine being, three persons who share it. The math isn't 1+1+1=3 gods but 1×1×1 — one "what," three "whos." Then let Scripture speak: at Jesus' baptism the Father speaks, the Son stands in the water, the Spirit descends (Matt. 3:16–17). And only a triune God can be eternally loving in himself — love needs a beloved (John 17:24).

3"Jesus never said, 'I am God — worship me.'"
The objectionShow me one verse where Jesus says those exact words. He called himself the Son of Man and prayed to God — a prophet, not God.
A response

Jesus claimed deity the way a first-century Jew would — by doing what only God does and taking what only God owns. He forgave sins against God ("Why does this man speak like that? He is blaspheming!" — Mark 2:5–7), claimed to be Lord of the Sabbath (Mark 2:28), said "before Abraham was, I AM" (John 8:58 — they picked up stones), and accepted worship, including Thomas's "My Lord and my God!" (John 20:28) without correction.

Even "Son of Man" is a divine claim: in Daniel 7:13–14 the Son of Man rides the clouds, receives worship, and rules forever — the very passage Jesus quoted at his trial, which the high priest called blasphemy (Mark 14:62–64). The title Muslims think is humble is the strongest claim of all.

4"How can God have a son? Does God have a wife?"
The objection"How can He have a son when He has no consort?" (Surah 6:101). It is not befitting for Allah to beget (Surah 19:35, 92).
A response

Agree immediately: the idea that God took a wife and produced offspring is blasphemous — and Christians reject it as firmly as Muslims do. "Son of God" is not biological; it describes an eternal relationship of love and likeness within God, not an event.

Note that the Quran's objection targets pagan notions of divine offspring (which existed in Arabia), not the Christian doctrine. Scripture uses "son" the way Arabic uses ibn in phrases like ibn al-sabil ("son of the road" = traveler) — relationship, not procreation. The Son is the eternal Word (John 1:1) — and interestingly, the Quran also calls Jesus God's Word (Surah 4:171).

5"How could God die on a cross? Who was running the universe?"
The objectionGod cannot die. If Jesus was God and Jesus died, then God died — which is absurd. And why would a just God punish an innocent man anyway?
A response

Death in Scripture is not cessation of existence — it's the separation of the person from the body. On the cross the divine nature didn't stop existing or stop sustaining the universe; the eternal Son, having taken on a human nature, experienced human death in that nature (Phil. 2:6–8). The Incarnation means one person, two natures.

On justice: the cross is not God punishing an unwilling third party. It is the Judge himself stepping down to absorb the sentence — "God was in Christ reconciling the world to himself" (2 Cor. 5:19). The one who pays the debt is the one owed it. That's not injustice; it's mercy at cost to the merciful — something the scales of deeds can never offer.

6"Paul corrupted Christianity — Jesus' real message was Islam."
The objectionJesus preached submission to God; Paul, who never met him, invented the deity of Christ, the cross, and salvation by grace.
A response

The core gospel predates Paul. In 1 Corinthians 15:3–7 Paul quotes a creed he "received" — Christ died for our sins, was buried, was raised, appeared — which scholars (including skeptics) date to within about five years of the crucifixion, before Paul's major ministry. Paul also submitted his message to Peter, James, and John in Jerusalem, "and they added nothing to me" (Gal. 2:1–9).

And the earliest sources of all — Mark's Gospel and the pre-Pauline hymns (Phil. 2:6–11) — already present a divine, crucified, risen Jesus. There is no early "Islamic Jesus" layer beneath the New Testament; that Jesus first appears in the 7th century.

Remember the goal: winning an argument is not winning a person. Answer honestly, admit what you don't know, and keep pointing back to the person of Jesus — the one figure both books cannot stop talking about.

One Jesus, Four Claims

Which Christianity?

Evangelical Protestants, Roman Catholics, Latter-day Saints, and Jehovah's Witnesses all claim to follow Jesus — and teach very different things about who he is and how he saves. This section compares all four from this site's evangelical vantage point, honestly: Rome is family with a Reformation-sized disagreement; the LDS and Watchtower restorations deny the historic core itself.

✝️ Evangelical ⛪ Roman Catholic 📖 Latter-day Saints 🗼 Jehovah's Witnesses
Authority Scripture alone (sola scriptura) — the 66-book Bible as the final rule; creeds and teachers serve under it. Scripture (73 books) plus Sacred Tradition, interpreted by the Magisterium — the Pope and bishops in communion with him. The Bible "as far as it is translated correctly," plus the Book of Mormon, D&C, and Pearl of Great Price — and a living prophet. The Bible (New World Translation) as interpreted solely by the Governing Body through Watchtower publications.

The firstborn spirit child of the Father — a distinct divine being and our elder brother, not eternally God. Jehovah's first creation, identified with Michael the archangel — "a god," but not Almighty God.
How salvation works By grace alone through faith alone; good works are the fruit of salvation, never the ground (Eph. 2:8–10). By grace through faith and the sacraments: justification begins at baptism, grows through cooperation, is lost by mortal sin, restored by penance. Christ's atonement plus ordinances and obedience — culminating in temple covenants and "all we can do" (2 Nephi 25:23). Faith in the ransom plus preaching, meetings, and loyalty to Jehovah's organization.

Christ — through the restored priesthood and temple ordinances, including proxy work for the dead. Christ mediates for the 144,000; the "other sheep" approach Jehovah through the organization.

⛪ Rome and the Reformation's question

⛪ Roman Catholic teaching

The Church Christ founded, with grace through sacraments

Rome claims unbroken succession from Peter (Matt. 16:18): Scripture and Sacred Tradition together, interpreted by the Magisterium. Grace ordinarily flows through seven sacraments, and the Mass re-presents Christ's one sacrifice to the faithful.

Justification is a lifelong process — infused at baptism, increased by cooperation with grace, forfeited by mortal sin, recovered through confession — with purgatory purifying whatever remains at death. Mary and the saints intercede for the faithful on the way.

CCC 846CCC 1992Trent, Session 6
✝️ The evangelical response

Family — with a Reformation-sized disagreement

Unlike the LDS and Watchtower systems, Rome confesses the Trinity, the deity of Christ, and his bodily resurrection — the historic core is genuinely shared, and evangelicals should say so gladly. The dispute is how Christ's finished work reaches the sinner.

Scripture describes justification as a verdict received by faith, not a lifelong infusion: God "justifies the ungodly" who believe (Rom. 4:5), so "there is therefore now no condemnation" (Rom. 8:1). Christ's single offering "has perfected for all time those who are being sanctified" (Heb. 10:14) — leaving no room for a repeated sacrifice, a treasury of merit, or purgatory. And with one mediator (1 Tim. 2:5), the believer needs no other intercessor.

📖 The Latter-day Saints

📖 LDS teaching

A restoration through Joseph Smith

In 1820 Joseph Smith reported a vision of the Father and the Son declaring every existing church wrong; through him the priesthood and true church were restored, and the Book of Mormon was translated from golden plates. A living prophet leads the church today.

God the Father is an exalted man with a glorified body (D&C 130:22); humans are his spirit children who may progress to exaltation — "As man now is, God once was; as God now is, man may be" (Lorenzo Snow). Salvation joins Christ's atonement to ordinances and obedience: "saved by grace, after all we can do" (2 Nephi 25:23).

D&C 130:222 Nephi 25:23Joseph Smith—History 1:19
✝️ Historic Christianity's response

No total apostasy, no second gospel

Jesus promised the gates of hell would not prevail against his church (Matt. 16:18) — a total apostasy contradicts his own promise. And Paul preemptively ruled out "another gospel," even one delivered by "an angel from heaven" (Gal. 1:8) — words worth pondering when the message comes via the angel Moroni.

Scripture knows one eternally unchanging God: "from everlasting to everlasting, you are God" (Ps. 90:2); "before me no god was formed, nor shall there be any after me" (Isa. 43:10). And salvation is grace alone — "not a result of works" (Eph. 2:8–9), not grace after all we can do.

Matt. 16:18Gal. 1:8Isa. 43:10Eph. 2:8–9
1If God was once a man, who was God before him?

Eternal progression implies an infinite regress of gods, each exalted by the one before. Ask gently how that fits Jehovah's own words: "Before me no god was formed, nor shall there be any after me" (Isa. 43:10), "Is there a God besides me? … I know not any" (Isa. 44:8), and "from everlasting to everlasting, you are God" (Ps. 90:2).

Why it matters: The doctrine of God is where LDS teaching departs furthest from the Bible — and, surprisingly, from the Book of Mormon itself (see the next question). It's the foundational issue beneath every other difference.
2Why does the Book of Mormon disagree with later LDS doctrine?

The Book of Mormon teaches one unchangeable, eternal God: "unchangeable from all eternity to all eternity" (Moroni 8:18); Amulek insists there is one God (Alma 11:26–29); Mormon 9:9–10 rebukes those who imagine a changing God. The plurality of gods, God's own progression, and human exaltation arrived later, in Joseph Smith's Nauvoo period (the King Follett discourse, 1844).

So the question: if the Book of Mormon is "the most correct of any book on earth," why did the prophet's later teaching move away from it?

Why it matters: A doctrine that develops away from its own founding scripture looks like a human trajectory, not a restoration of ancient truth.
3Where did the Book of Mormon happen?

No city, coin, or inscription from the Nephite or Lamanite civilizations has ever been identified — nor evidence of the steel, chariots, wheat, and horses the book places in pre-Columbian America. Mainstream DNA research finds Native American ancestry in Asia, not ancient Israel.

Contrast the Bible: Jerusalem, the Pilate stone, the Caiaphas ossuary, Hezekiah's tunnel — biblical archaeology fills museums, and Luke opens his Gospel inviting verification (Luke 1:1–4).

Why it matters: A restoration of real history should leave real traces. The gospel invites checking; so should any book claiming to complete it.
4Can a burning in the bosom settle what's true?

Moroni 10:4–5 invites the reader to pray for a feeling of confirmation. But sincere Muslims, Hindus, and others report equally powerful spiritual experiences confirming contradictory claims — a feeling can confirm commitment, but it cannot adjudicate history.

Scripture points elsewhere: the Bereans were commended for examining the Scriptures to see if the message was true (Acts 17:11), and Jeremiah warns "the heart is deceitful above all things" (Jer. 17:9).

Why it matters: The ground of faith matters as much as its sincerity. Testimony should rest on what God has done in checkable history, not on an inner sensation every religion can produce.
5Is grace sufficient — or only after all you can do?

2 Nephi 25:23 conditions grace on prior effort, and Moroni 10:32 says "deny yourselves of all ungodliness … then is his grace sufficient for you." Paul runs the order the other way: "to the one who does not work but believes in him who justifies the ungodly, his faith is counted as righteousness" (Rom. 4:5).

Ask the personal version: have you done "all you can do"? How would you know?

Why it matters: This is the most heart-level question for LDS friends, many of whom carry real exhaustion under worthiness culture. The gospel's word is done — "It is finished" (John 19:30).

🗼 The Jehovah's Witnesses

🗼 Watchtower teaching

Jehovah's organization and a created Christ

Charles Taze Russell's Bible Students (1870s) became the Watchtower Society: Christendom apostatized, the Trinity is pagan, and Jehovah directs one visible organization through its Governing Body. Jesus is Jehovah's first creation — identified with Michael the archangel — who was raised a spirit creature and began ruling invisibly in 1914.

Salvation means exercising faith, preaching, and staying loyal to the organization. Heaven is for 144,000 "anointed"; the "other sheep" hope to live forever on a paradise earth. There is no immortal soul and no hell — the wicked are annihilated.

John 1:1 (NWT)Rev. 7:4 (as read)Watchtower publications
✝️ Historic Christianity's response

The earliest Christians worshiped Jesus

Thomas confesses "My Lord and my God!" and Jesus blesses rather than corrects him (John 20:28–29). God commands "let all God's angels worship him" (Heb. 1:6) — impossible if he is an angel. He bears Jehovah's own exclusive titles: the First and the Last (Isa. 44:6; Rev. 1:17), the Alpha and the Omega (Rev. 22:13).

And access to God runs through a person, not an organization: "there is one mediator between God and men, the man Christ Jesus, who gave himself as a ransom for all" (1 Tim. 2:5–6) — with one hope offered to whoever believes (John 3:16).

John 20:28Heb. 1:6Rev. 22:131 Tim. 2:5–6
1Who is the Alpha and the Omega?

In Revelation 1:8, Jehovah declares himself the Alpha and the Omega. In Revelation 22:12–13, the one who is "coming quickly" to repay everyone says: "I am the Alpha and the Omega, the first and the last." And in Revelation 1:17–18, the First and the Last is the one who "was dead and came to life."

Ask: when did Jehovah die? Or is Jesus bearing Jehovah's own exclusive title (cf. Isa. 44:6 — "I am the first and I am the last; besides me there is no god")?

Why it matters: Within the book of Revelation alone — in the Watchtower's own translation — Jesus carries the titles Jehovah says belong to no one else.
2If Jehovah created "alone," how did he create through a creature?

Isaiah 44:24: "I am the LORD, who made all things, who alone stretched out the heavens, who spread out the earth by myself." Yet Colossians 1:16–17 says all things were created through and for the Son — which is why the New World Translation inserts the word "[other]" four times ("all [other] things"), with no basis in the Greek text.

John 1:3 closes the loophole: "without him was not any thing made that was made." If the Son were himself made, he would have had to make himself.

Why it matters: The creation texts are the cleanest test of the "first creation" doctrine — and the translation had to be altered to survive them.
3What should we do with the failed dates?

The Society taught Christ's presence began in 1874, then moved it to 1914; announced "Millions Now Living Will Never Die" for 1925 (the patriarchs would return); and built worldwide expectation around 1975. Each date passed and was reinterpreted.

Deuteronomy 18:21–22 gives the test: if a word spoken in God's name does not come to pass, God did not speak it. Ask kindly: how should that test apply to an organization claiming to be God's sole channel?

Why it matters: The organization's authority is the load-bearing wall of Witness faith — everything else (the 144,000, the blood ban, the NWT) rests on it.
4Does the New World Translation hold up?

The NWT was produced by an anonymous committee with no published credentials in biblical languages. It renders John 1:1 "the Word was a god" — yet doesn't apply the same grammatical rule to the identical constructions in John 1:6, 12, 13, and 18. It inserts "Jehovah" 237 times into the New Testament with zero manuscript support, and adds "[other]" to Colossians 1.

Greek scholars across the spectrum — including Bruce Metzger, the twentieth century's leading textual critic — publicly rejected these renderings.

Why it matters: The distinctive doctrines lean on the distinctive translation. If the translation can't stand on the Greek, neither can what's built on it.
5Is Jesus your mediator — or only theirs?

Watchtower teaching applies the new covenant and Christ's mediation to the 144,000 anointed; the "great crowd" benefits only indirectly, through the organization. But Paul writes: "there is one mediator between God and men … who gave himself as a ransom for all" (1 Tim. 2:5–6), and Jesus says "no one comes to the Father except through me" (John 14:6) — through him, not through a channel.

The personal question: do you have direct access to God, and can you know you have everlasting life (1 John 5:13)?

Why it matters: This is where doctrine becomes personal — the gospel offers every believer what the Watchtower reserves for a class of 144,000.

The questions they'll ask you

Fair is fair — Catholics, Latter-day Saints, and Witnesses have sincere questions for evangelicals too. Here are the seven you'll hear most, with responses.

⛪"Where was your church before Luther — and didn't the Catholic Church give you the Bible?"
The questionIf salvation is by faith alone, where were the faith-alone Christians for 1,500 years? And you received your Bible from Catholic councils — how can you use it against the church that gave it to you?
An evangelical response

The church did not create the canon; it recognized books that already carried God's authority — the way a scale recognizes weight it didn't create. The core of the New Testament was functioning as Scripture centuries before any council listed it (the Muratorian fragment, c. AD 170, already lists most of it).

And Christ's people were never absent: the gospel of grace runs through Augustine, through medieval voices, to Reformers who claimed to recover, not invent. The church was wherever the gospel was believed — "where two or three are gathered in my name" (Matt. 18:20).

⛪"Doesn't James say faith without works is dead?"
The questionJames 2:24 — "a person is justified by works and not by faith alone." The only place the Bible says "faith alone," it says not by faith alone. Doesn't that settle it?
An evangelical response

James and Paul aim at different errors. Paul targets earning God's verdict: God "justifies the ungodly" who believe, apart from works (Rom. 4:5). James targets claiming an empty "faith" that never acts — demons "believe" that way (Jas. 2:19). The faith that saves is never alone; it "works through love" (Gal. 5:6).

Notice James's own example: Abraham was counted righteous when he believed (Gen. 15:6) — decades before the deed James cites (Gen. 22). Works vindicated before people what faith had already received from God.

⛪"Thirty thousand denominations — hasn't sola scriptura failed?"
The questionWithout a magisterium, everyone becomes his own pope. Look at Protestant fragmentation — doesn't the confusion prove you need an infallible interpreter?
An evangelical response

The famous number is wildly inflated — it counts national branches of the same tradition as separate "denominations." Confessional Protestants share striking unity on the essentials: Trinity, incarnation, atonement, resurrection, salvation by grace. Meanwhile Rome's own umbrella covers comparable diversity of belief in practice.

An infallible interpreter doesn't end interpretation anyway — Catholics still dispute what councils and encyclicals mean. The unity Jesus prayed for is unity in truth ("Sanctify them in the truth; your word is truth," John 17:17), built by shared submission to God's word, not by an org chart.

📖"God spoke through prophets before — why not today? Why fear more scripture?"
The questionYou believe God called Moses and Isaiah. Why would a loving God go silent? Shouldn't you at least test whether he called Joseph Smith too?
An evangelical response

Hebrews answers directly: "Long ago God spoke by the prophets, but in these last days he has spoken to us by his Son" (Heb. 1:1–2). Jesus is God's final word; the apostles' testimony completes it — the faith was "once for all delivered to the saints" (Jude 3), and the church is "built on the foundation of the apostles and prophets" (Eph. 2:20). Foundations aren't relaid.

So the test isn't whether God could speak again — it's whether the new message agrees with the once-given gospel. Paul's standard: "even if we or an angel from heaven should preach a gospel contrary… let him be accursed" (Gal. 1:8). A restoration that changes who God is fails that test.

📖"With thousands of churches disagreeing, doesn't that prove the Great Apostasy?"
The questionJoseph Smith asked which church was right because they all disagreed. Doesn't the confusion itself show the priesthood was lost and a restoration was needed?
An evangelical response

Disagreement proves human fallibility, not divine abandonment. Jesus promised the gates of hell would not prevail against his church (Matt. 16:18) and pledged "I am with you always, to the end of the age" (Matt. 28:20). A total apostasy doesn't just criticize churches — it makes Jesus' own promises fail.

And Christians have disagreed about secondary things from the beginning (Acts 15) while confessing "one Lord, one faith, one baptism" (Eph. 4:5). The remedy Scripture prescribes is testing everything against the word (Acts 17:11) — not a new priesthood and a new gospel.

🗼"The word 'Trinity' isn't in the Bible — isn't it a pagan idea?"
The questionShow me the verse that says "Trinity." Constantine's council invented it in 325, borrowing from pagan triads. Jehovah is one, not three.
An evangelical response

The word is absent; the data is everywhere: the Father is God (Gal. 1:1), the Son is God (John 1:1; 20:28), the Spirit is God (Acts 5:3–4 — lying to the Spirit is lying to God), and yet God is one (Deut. 6:4). Jesus commands baptism "in the name" — singular — "of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit" (Matt. 28:19). The doctrine is just refusing to delete any of those verses. (The word "Bible" isn't in the Bible either.)

And pagan triads were three separate gods — precisely what the Trinity is not. Nicaea didn't invent the belief; it defended what churches were already confessing against a new teaching that Christ was a creature — the very position the Watchtower revived.

🗼"If Jesus is God, whom was he praying to? Did God die?"
The questionJesus prayed to the Father, said "the Father is greater than I," and died. God can't pray to himself or die. Doesn't that prove Jesus isn't God?
An evangelical response

The Son prayed to the Father — two persons in communion, which is exactly what the Trinity teaches; it would only be a problem if the doctrine said Jesus and the Father were the same person. "The Father is greater than I" (John 14:28) speaks of the incarnate Son's position, not his nature — the one who "was in the form of God" had "emptied himself, taking the form of a servant" (Phil. 2:6–7).

And death is separation, not cessation: the eternal Son, having taken a human nature, experienced human death in that nature, while "in him all things hold together" (Col. 1:17) remained true. The one who died is the person who is God the Son — which is why his death can pay for the world (Acts 20:28: "the church of God, which he obtained with his own blood").

A word on posture: Catholic friends are family in the creeds even where the Reformation divide is real — argue like family, not enemies. And the missionaries and Witnesses at your door are usually sincere, disciplined, and braced for hostility; kindness disarms more than cleverness. Offer them time, ask real questions, and keep pointing to the Jesus of the Gospels: "always being prepared to make a defense … with gentleness and respect" (1 Pet. 3:15).

The Sixth Worldview

Atheism ↔ Christianity

Atheism is a worldview too — it makes claims about reality, morality, and meaning that can be examined like any other. Here's the head-to-head, followed by atheism's biggest objections to Christianity and the questions apologists ask back.

✝️ Christianity

A universe with an Author

Reality begins with mind, not matter: "In the beginning, God created" (Gen. 1:1). The universe had a cause; its fine-tuning reflects design; moral law reflects a moral Lawgiver; and human reason works because we're made in the image of a rational God.

Meaning, dignity, and hope are discovered, not invented — grounded in something that outlasts the sun.

Gen. 1:1Rom. 1:20Rom. 2:14–15
⚛️ Atheism

A universe without one

Matter and energy are the whole story. The universe either always existed, arose from a quantum vacuum, or is one of countless multiverse bubbles. Life emerged by chemistry, minds by evolution, and morality by social instinct.

As Richard Dawkins puts it, the universe has "no design, no purpose, no evil, no good, nothing but blind, pitiless indifference." Meaning is a human project — real to us, invisible to the cosmos.

Dawkins, River Out of Eden

The hinge: Frank Turek's point in I Don't Have Enough Faith to Be an Atheist — both views take steps beyond the lab. The question is which worldview better explains the data everyone shares: a universe that began, laws that are finely tuned, minds that reason, and morals that feel binding.

Atheism's objections — and responses

The four objections you'll hear most, with responses drawn from The Gospel Coalition and CrossExamined.

1"A good God wouldn't allow so much evil and suffering."
The objectionIf God is all-good and all-powerful, evil shouldn't exist. It does. So God is either not good, not powerful, or not there.
A response

First, the hidden premise: calling anything genuinely evil assumes an objective standard of good — and a standard above humanity is exactly what atheism denies. The objection borrows the very yardstick it's trying to throw away.

Second, Christian theodicy: God permits evil he intends to defeat — free creatures can genuinely love only if they can genuinely refuse (the free-will theodicy), and God's track record is turning the worst evil into the greatest good: the cross itself (Acts 2:23). Christianity is the one worldview where God doesn't watch suffering from a distance — he entered it.

2"Okay — then who made God?"
The objectionIf everything needs a cause, God needs one too. Saying "God just exists" is special pleading.
A response

The Kalam argument never claims "everything needs a cause" — it claims everything that begins to exist needs one. The universe began (Big Bang cosmology, the second law of thermodynamics); God, by definition, did not. An eternal, uncaused being isn't special pleading — it's the terminus every chain of causes logically requires. Even the atheist needs something eternal; the question is whether it's mindless matter or a mind.

3"Science has explained God away."
The objectionLightning, disease, evolution — every gap God used to fill, science filled instead. Faith is what you have before you have data.
A response

Christians agree God isn't a gap-filler — he's the reason there's an ordered, mathematically describable universe for science to study at all. Modern science was largely built by theists (Kepler, Newton, Faraday, Maxwell, Lemaître — a priest — proposed the Big Bang) who expected laws because they expected a Lawgiver.

And science keeps uncovering things it can't explain from inside: why the constants of physics are fine-tuned for life to one part in trillions, why there's something rather than nothing, and why blind neurons should produce reasoning we can trust. Explaining how the engine runs doesn't explain away the engineer.

4"Faith is belief without evidence."
The objectionReligion asks you to believe things on authority; science asks you to check. Faith and reason are opposites.
A response

That defines faith in a way the Bible doesn't. Biblical faith is trust based on evidence — Luke opens his Gospel citing eyewitness investigation (Luke 1:1–4); Paul stakes everything on a checkable public event with named living witnesses (1 Cor. 15:3–8); Jesus offered his wounds to a doubter (John 20:27). "Come now, let us reason together," says the LORD (Isa. 1:18).

Everyone exercises trust beyond direct proof — in memory, in other minds, in the uniformity of nature. The question is never faith vs. no faith; it's which object of trust holds the evidence.

Four questions to ask your atheist friends

The classic apologetic arguments, phrased as friendly questions.

01

Why is there something rather than nothing?

Whatever begins to exist has a cause; the universe began to exist. What caused it — and why does "nothing" never seem to mean nothing in the naturalistic answers?

02

Why is the universe fine-tuned for life?

Change the cosmological constant or the strong force a hair and no stars, chemistry, or life exist. Design, necessity, or an unobservable multiverse — which takes more faith?

03

Is anything really wrong?

If morality is only evolution's survival software, was the Holocaust just unfashionable? Objective moral duties need a ground taller than us — the moral argument in one question.

04

Why trust a brain that evolution built for survival, not truth?

If thoughts are just chemistry selected for fitness, why trust the chemistry that produced atheism? Reason itself testifies to a rational source (Darwin's own "horrid doubt").

And the same posture applies: most atheists left a church that answered questions badly, or never met a Christian who welcomed hard ones. Curiosity beats combat — "always being prepared to make a defense… with gentleness and respect" (1 Pet. 3:15).
